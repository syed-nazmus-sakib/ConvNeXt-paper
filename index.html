  • 2014–15 — VGG, Inception, ResNet. Depth, 3×3 stacks, and residual connections made very deep networks trainable.

Motivation & Context

Why this paper had to be written

2020 — the Transformer earthquake

  • ViT (2020). Cut an image into 16×16 patches, feed them to a pure Transformer. With enough data (JFT-300M) it beat the best ConvNets — with no convolution at all.
  • Swin Transformer (2021). Brought back hierarchy and local windows of attention — becoming a general backbone that topped detection & segmentation.
  • The narrative hardened: attention is the future; convolution is legacy.
  • But notice the irony — Swin's wins came from re-introducing ConvNet priors (locality, hierarchy, sliding windows) back into the Transformer.

If Swin had to borrow from ConvNets to win… how much was really attention?

The question ConvNeXt asks

A Swin Transformer differs from a ResNet in two ways at once — and they were always changed together:

1
The operator

A new self-attention layer replaces the convolution.

2
Everything else

A modern training recipe and macro design.

Everyone credited the operator.  Nobody isolated the recipe.

“How much of the gap survives if we modernize a pure ConvNet — and never touch the operator?”

So the authors take a ResNet-50 and walk it, step by step, toward a Swin Transformer.

What if we change nothing but the way we train it?

76.1%→78.8%+2.7

Same ResNet-50 — not one layer touched. We only swap in a 2020s training recipe:

  • Train it far longer — 90 → 300 epochs, with the AdamW optimizer.
  • Pile on augmentation — Mixup, CutMix, RandAugment, Random Erasing.
  • Regularize the depth — Stochastic Depth and Label Smoothing.
The most honest — and most ignored — result
  • More than half of the ResNet's "gap" to Swin was never about the operator at all.
  • It was the recipe. The old ConvNets weren't out-designed — they were just under-trained.
  • So this is the fair starting line. Every architecture change later is measured on top of it.

Inside the recipe · data augmentation

Show the network many more “views” of each image, so it can't memorize — it must generalize.

🐱🐶
Mixup
How · blend two images and their labels: 0.6·cat + 0.4·dog.
Why · forces linear behaviour between classes → smoother decision boundaries, less over-confidence.
🐱🐶
CutMix
How · paste a rectangular patch of image B into image A; mix labels by patch area.
Why · keeps real, sharp pixels (no blur) while still teaching the net to handle partial objects.
🐱
RandAugment
How · apply a few random transforms — rotate, shear, colour, contrast — at a fixed magnitude.
Why · huge, cheap data diversity with almost no hyper-parameters to tune.
🐱
Random Erasing
How · blank out a random rectangle of the image during training.
Why · the net can't rely on one give-away patch → robust to occlusion & clutter.

Inside the recipe · Stochastic Depth

how a 300-epoch, very deep network avoids over-fitting

  • A residual block gives the signal two routes: through its layers, or around them on the identity skip.
  • The trick: on every mini-batch, flip a coin per block and drop some of them entirely — the block becomes a plain pass-through (deeper blocks dropped more often).
  • So each step trains a different, shallower network. You're quietly training a whole ensemble — and the full-depth model at test time inherits all of it.
  • Shorter expected paths also keep gradients healthy.
live: blocks dropping each step

Inside the recipe · Label Smoothing

calibrating what we ask the network to predict

  • We normally train against a one-hot label: the true class is 100%, every other class is exactly 0%.
  • That asks the network to be infinitely confident — the logits keep growing and it clings too hard to the training data.
  • Soften the target instead: give the true class most of the mass (ConvNeXt uses 90%) and share the rest across the others.
  • A little built-in doubt → better-calibrated probabilities and stronger generalization.

What if the depth is in the wrong stage?

78.8 → 79.4  ·  Macro design ①

Four stages, one fixed budget

A ResNet sees an image in four stages, halving the resolution at each. Inside a stage it just repeats the same block — and ResNet-50's split is (3, 4, 6, 3), sixteen blocks in all.

Borrow the Transformer's split

Swin pours most of its blocks into the third stage — a 1 : 1 : 3 : 1 ratio. ConvNeXt simply copies that, shifting blocks out of stage ② into stage ③: (3, 3, 9, 3). Same depth, same ≈4.5G FLOPs — only the distribution moves.

The third stage does the thinking

Stage ③ runs at 14×14: cheap enough to stack deep, yet coarse enough to reason about object parts rather than edges. Depth spent here buys more accuracy per FLOP than anywhere else.

What if we cut the image into clean patches?

79.4 → 79.5  ·  Macro design ②

What a stem even does

Before any real processing, the stem compresses the raw 224×224 image into a smaller feature map that the rest of the network builds on.

Overlap, or clean tiles?

ResNet's stem is a 7×7 conv at stride 2, then a max-pool — its windows overlap and it shrinks the image in two coarse steps. ConvNeXt swaps in a single 4×4, stride-4 conv: every window is a clean, non-overlapping patch — exactly how ViT slices an image into tokens.

Speaking the Transformer's language

The accuracy move is tiny, but the point isn't the number — the ConvNet now ingests pixels the same way as the Transformer it's chasing. Simpler, faster, and a fair, apples-to-apples start.

→
each patch is flattened into one token → a sequence, like ViT

What if one convolution is doing two jobs at once?

78.8 → 80.5  ·  Macro design ③ — the conceptual heart

One conv, two tangled jobs

A normal convolution does two things in a single step: it blends neighbouring pixels (space) and sums across every input channel (features). Powerful — but expensive, and the two roles are fused together.

Factor it: where, then what

Depthwise-separable conv splits that in two. A depthwise conv gives each channel its own filter, so it mixes only over space. A 1×1 “pointwise” conv then mixes only across channels. Same expressive power, a fraction of the FLOPs.

Spend the savings on width

Depthwise is so cheap that ConvNeXt reinvests the budget — widening the net 64 → 96 channels to match Swin. This pairing is the biggest single jump of the macro section.

The Transformer did this all along

Attention mixes across space; the MLP mixes across channels. Depthwise + pointwise is the convolutional echo of exactly that split — which is why the two families quietly converge.

What if we widen the middle instead of pinching it?

80.5 → 80.6  ·  Macro design ④  ·  from MobileNetV2

The classic bottleneck

A ResNet block pinches the channels down in the middle — many → few → many — so the costly 3×3 runs on few channels. Cheap, but the representation is squeezed through a narrow neck.

Flip it inside-out

The inverted residual does the opposite: few → many → few. It expands the channels ~4× in the middle, does the spatial mixing there (now a cheap depthwise conv), then projects back down.

Counter-intuitively, it's leaner

Because the spatial op is depthwise, the wide middle is cheap; the only heavy 1×1's sit on the thin ends. In the figure, the same block drops from 1.1M → 52K weights.

It's literally the Transformer's MLP

A Transformer's feed-forward block expands the dimension 4× (96 → 384), applies a nonlinearity, and projects back to 96 — an inverted bottleneck. ConvNeXt's block and the FFN are the same shape.

Why has the kernel been stuck at 3×3?

80.6 → 80.6  ·  Macro design ⑤

A decade of 3×3 dogma

Since VGG, ConvNets stack tiny 3×3 kernels. But a Transformer's local attention looks at a whole 7×7 neighbourhood at once — a much wider view per layer.

Move the depthwise conv up first

To make big kernels affordable, ConvNeXt slides the depthwise layer up, ahead of the 1×1's, so it runs on fewer channels. A small dip (80.6 → 79.9) — but large kernels are now cheap.

Grow the window to 7×7

Enlarge the depthwise kernel 3 → 5 → 7 (79.9 → 80.6). Each layer now sees the same span as Swin's local-attention window — the last big conceptual gap to the Transformer, closed.

And it stops at 7

Push to 9 or 11 and accuracy flatlines while cost climbs. Seven is the sweet spot — an honest, empirical limit.

What if the block needs far fewer parts?

80.6 → 81.5  ·  Micro design

Borrow the Transformer's sparsity

A ResNet block crams a norm and an activation after almost every layer. A Transformer block is strikingly bare — one activation, one norm. Each extra piece can disrupt the signal, so ConvNeXt strips the block down to match.

A smoother switch: ReLU → GELU

GELU is a smooth ReLU — it doesn't hard-cut at zero, letting a little negative signal (and a smooth gradient) through. The swap alone is accuracy-neutral, but keeping just one GELU per block lifts 80.6 → 81.3.

Fewer, better norms: BN → LayerNorm

Drop to one norm per block (81.3 → 81.4), then swap BatchNorm for LayerNorm (81.4 → 81.5). LayerNorm normalizes each sample on its own — so it stays stable at the tiny batch sizes used for detection & segmentation.

The finishing move — and the full journey

81.5 → 82.0  ·  separate downsampling

Pull downsampling out of the block

ResNet shrinks resolution inside a residual block. ConvNeXt gives it a dedicated layer between stages — a 2×2, stride-2 conv with its own LayerNorm.

Why it earns its own layer

Untangling “change resolution” from “compute features” keeps training stable — a norm guards each resolution change, exactly like Swin's patch-merging. The last step: 81.5 → 82.0.

Experimental Results & Impact

Does the modernized ConvNet actually deliver?

ImageNet-1K — matching or beating Swin

  • ConvNeXt-T 82.1% vs Swin-T 81.3% (4.5G).
  • ConvNeXt-S 83.1% vs Swin-S 83.0%.
  • ConvNeXt-B 83.8% vs Swin-B 83.5%.
  • Wins at every size — and at higher throughput than the matched Swin.

Same compute budget, consistently ahead.

Scaling up — the accuracy / compute frontier

  • Across the whole curve, ConvNeXt sits on or above the Transformer frontier.
  • Pre-train on ImageNet-22K and the gains hold at scale — the old "ConvNets don't scale with data" worry does not appear.
  • ConvNeXt-XL reaches 87.8% (22K-pretrained, 384²).

Beyond classification — detection & segmentation

  • As a backbone, ConvNeXt matches or beats Swin on box AP, mask AP and mIoU — the very tasks Swin was built to win.
  • The gains transfer to real downstream vision, not just classification.

Conclusion & Critical Reflection

What it proved — and what it left open

The verdict

What it proved
  • The operator was never the whole story. A modernized pure ConvNet matches a Vision Transformer at equal compute — classification, detection, segmentation.
  • Progress was confounded. New models change operator and recipe at once; ConvNeXt's controlled walk separated them and credited each fairly.
  • Inductive bias still pays. Locality & hierarchy keep it efficient even without massive data — the priors ViT discarded were useful after all.
The honest caveats
  • No new primitive. Masterful engineering & curation, not invention — and the biggest jump came from the recipe, not architecture.
  • Tuned toward Swin. The roadmap optimizes to match one target — a different goal might yield a different "best".
  • Not the last word. Attention–conv hybrids remain strong; this is a reset, not a finish line.

ConvNets and Transformers borrowed each other's best ideas.

One last tribute — goodbye to convolutions

ConvNeXt was the last great push to prove the ConvNet still belongs — and it worked. Convolutions solved vision, and showed the world that deep learning could be trusted.

But by 2026 the field has moved on: the frontier is ViT-based, trained jointly on image + text, and open-vocabulary.

So here's one last bow to the architecture that carried vision for a decade.

Thank you.
